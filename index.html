Machine-Learning Crystal Ball. Who'll coronate the 2016 NCAA Champion?

Foretell the outcomes of the 2016 NCAA Basketball Tournament without divine inspiration

In this project I proposed a novel solution to Kaggle competition March Machine Learning Mania 2016 which, though completed when my collaborators and I participated, still accepts and scores submissions. The problem was to extrapolate the outcomes of all possible pair matches amongst 68 participating teams of NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Tournament in 2016, by looking at past three decades' match history. Given the outcomes are expressed in winning probabilities of one team against the other, Cross Entrophy was employed to score submissions by assessing how congruent the observed and the predicted probabilities are. Our concerted efforts yielded a score that would have ranked 3rd in the final leaderboard and entitled us to a $4,000 cash prize.

This problem really posed a challenge to machine learning models' capacity - that is, how applicable a model is to a variety of configurations. To predict how likely Team A will beat Team B in a match is inherently hard, since many latent factors could potentially sway the outcome. A straightforward yet simple-minded solution would be to count on the two teams' past battles: if Team A had more victories than Team B, then Team A would likely win. However, for a large tournament like NCAA, triumphs and fiascoes often happen in an interweaved manner. While Team A could have remark­able records against Team B, it might well loose to Team C that was constantly beaten by Team B. To complicate things further, not all teams have played with one another before! How well a model can fill in the blank spots in history is really a manifestation to its generalising capacity.

Realising these pitfalls, we shunned this idea of "local thinking". Instead, in the solution I propounded, the focus was on assessing the skill of each team, which was demonstrated throughout the match history, in a global context encompassing all teams. Whilst comparable amongst all teams, the skill of a team also absorbed the impacts of complex historical matches into a single measure. In this way, not only did we avoid disentangling match results, unprecedented matches also became predictable. My solution reused an implementation of TrueSkill, a Bayesian rating model developed by Microsoft to rank XBox players. The model maintains beliefs about the true skill of each team, represented by a Gaussian distribution N~(μ, σ) with μ denoting the expected skill level and σ denoting uncertainties about the skill. The skill of each team is adjusted given a new match outcome, the magnitude of which depends on how upsetting an outcome is: if Team A's victory is deemed certain and Team A wins, Team A's skill will scarcely increase; however, it will be significantly reduced in light of Team B's startling victory. The headline video gives an example of the learning process: notice how μ spreads and σ shrinks as TrueSkill gleans more knowledge.

However, a bare-bones TrueSkill model would only lead to a 13rd. The quantum leap to the 3rd was really achieved when I ingeniously argumented TrueSkill with addtional features unique to this problem. It all began with a mind-blowing question: if Team A scored more than Team B in a basketball game, did Team A really win Team B? While Team A certainly won by rule, it might not by skill. Two teams could really tie for their skills so that Team A only led by a tiny margin. Or Team A might have played at its home court, well received by its audience. We incorporated these factors into the final scores by subtracting certain amount from a score when a team was granted an advantage. There were also other facts indicative of two teams' skill levels. For example, if Team A won by a very large margin, then its skill might overshadow Team B's. Since TrueSkill doesn't take into account victory margins, we treated Team A as if it had multiple victories. On the contrary, if Team A suffered from considerable turnovers - that is, losing possessions of the ball - its score might be deducted to reflect a negative opinion on its skill. With the manipulation of scores, a match outcome may be completely flipped over before seen by TrueSkill, yet in doing so we obtained more realistic insights into each team's true skill, which finally earned us a 3rd place.

Struggle of the Captive. Who

Design a robot with sensory apparatus to explore a randomly constructed maze and to traverse between two designated points in the least possible amount of time

In case you're not familiar with The Little Prince, it's actually a French novella Le Petit Prince written by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry.

My contributions to this project are mainly on the following two core functions.

  • A robot controller to issue instructions. Our robot is like a toddler: it can walk around and sense the world but relies on the controller to tell it what to do. The controller is connected to the robot remotely via Transmission Control Protocol. Moreover, it provides a robot simulator (as shown above) that issues commands to a virtual robot, which allowed us to quickly test alternative algorithms before the physical robot was built.

  • An intelligent exploration algorithm based on Depth-first search. When the robot gets stuck in the maze, it needs to backtrack. It's easier to implement backtracking in software by just poping from the stack which in hardware, however, means the robot has to physically (costly) reverse along the previous path. As the maze gets explored, a shorter backtracking path may exist. Therefore, we used A* search algorithm to find such a path, which saved us tremendous time.

First Android™ Project. Naïve but practical.

Develop an Android application to allow patients to manage their appointments at ease

Patient Appointment Management System (PAMS) aims to create a hassle-free platform to foster better physician-patient communication. Its benefits are twofold:

  • Patients are automatically assigned available physicians. They can reschedule and receive updates on their appointments. Given that some treatments are partially ordered (like some ultrasound tests go before urine tests), PAMS prevents patients from jumping ahead treatments.

  • Physicians are able to view the medical records of a patient and schedule a referal appointment for a patient with another physician as the treatment procedure goes.

I worked on the login and signup functions as well as wrote a module to standardise communications with our backend server. Messages are sent to the server via HTTP requests in json format. Check out our PAMS-Android-Frontend and PAMS-Django-Backend. Of course, our video as well.

Deep Convolutional Neural Network. How AlphaGo beat Mr. Lee Sedol

Apply deep convolutional neural networks and autoencoders to recognise hand-written digits

This project is a mining of the MNIST database which contains images of hand-written digits in various shapes and orientations. We used 5,000 training images and 1,000 test images, eash of which has a 28x28 dimension. The experiment consists of two parts:

  • A deep convolutional neural network. We used two convolution layers of size 70 and 50, respectively, two mean subsampling layers, one fully connected layer and one softmax layer with 10 neurons as the output layer. The test accuracy was around 89%.

  • A stacked autoencoder. In an unsupervised fashion, we first trained two autoencoders of size 120 and 60, respectively, to extract features from the images. Then we trained a softmax output layer using a supervised learning algorithm. The test accuracy was around 93%.
