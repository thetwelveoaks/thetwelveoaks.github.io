Machine-Learning Crystal Ball. Who will coronate the 2016 NCAA Champion?

Foretell the outcomes of the 2016 NCAA Basketball Tournament without divine inspiration

In this project I proposed a novel solution to Kaggle competition March Machine Learning Mania 2016 which, though completed when my collaborators and I participated, still accepts and scores submissions. The problem was to extrapolate the outcomes of all possible pair matches amongst the 68 participating teams of NCAA Division I Men's Basketball Tournament in 2016, by looking at past three decades' match history. Our concerted efforts yielded a score that would have ranked 3rd in the final leaderboard and entitled us to a $4,000 cash prize.

This problem really challenges a machine-learning model's capacity - that is, how applicable a model is to a variety of configurations. To predict how likely Team A will beat Team B in a match is inherently hard, since many latent factors could potentially sway the outcome. A straightforward yet simple-minded solution would be to count on the two teams' past battles: if Team A had more victories than Team B, then Team A would likely win the next. However, for a large tournament like NCAA, triumphs and fiascoes often happen in an interweaved manner. While Team A could have remark­able records against Team B, it might lose to Team C that was constantly beaten by Team B. Moreover, not all teams have played with one another before! How well a model can fill in the blank spots in history is really a manifestation to its generalising capacity.

Realising these pitfalls, we shunned the idea of "local thinking"; instead, in the solution I propounded, the focus was on assessing the skill of each team in the context of all the teams. The skill of a team provides a comparable and comprehensive measure that takes into account the impacts of complex historical matches. Not only did we avoid bogging down in the match details, unprecedented matches also became predictable. My solution was built upon TrueSkill, a Bayesian rating model developed by Microsoft to rank XBox players. The model maintains beliefs about the true skill of each team, represented by a Gaussian distribution N~(μ, σ) with μ denoting the expected skill and σ denoting the confidence about that skill. The skill of each team is adjusted given a new match outcome, the magnitude of which depends on how upsetting an outcome is: if Team A's victory is deemed certain and Team A wins, Team A's skill will scarcely increase; however, it will be significantly reduced in light of Team B's startling victory. The headline video shows the learning process: μ spreads and the curve becomes taller as σ shrinks and TrueSkill becomes more confident.

However, a bare-bones TrueSkill model would only lead to a 13rd. The quantum leap to the 3rd was really achieved when I ingeniously argumented TrueSkill with addtional features unique to this problem. It all began with a mind-blowing question: if Team A scored more than Team B in a basketball game, did Team A really win Team B? While Team A certainly won by rule, it might not by skill. Two teams could really tie for their skills so that Team A only led by a tiny margin. Or Team A might have played at its home court, well received by its audience. I incorporated these factors into the final scores by subtracting certain amount from a score when a team was granted an advantage. There were also other indicators of two teams' skill levels. For example, if Team A won by a very large margin, its skill should be far above Team B's. Since TrueSkill doesn't consider victory margins, Team A would be treated as if it had multiple victories. On the contrary, if Team A suffered from considerable turnovers - that is, losing possessions of the ball - its score might be deducted to reflect a negative opinion on its skill. With this creative manipulation of scores, a match outcome may be completely flipped over before seen by TrueSkill, yet in doing so we obtained more realistic insights into each team's true skill, which finally earned us a 3rd place.

A Kaleidoscope of Roads to Rome. Which is the shortest at 7AM?

Determine the route with the least travel time at different hours of a day

Our everyday experiences suggest the travel time between two locations usually varies with time: a 20-min trip in the afternoon take an hour during the morning rush. Therefore, it is impossible to figure out the best route with minimal travel time without reference to this time-dependent pattern. While monitoring the current traffic certainly helps, this project aims to extract insights from historical GPS trajectories of thousands of taxis. The success of this idea hinges upon the assumption that taxi drivers, with extensive driving experiences, have accumulated abundant but subliminal knowledge about public roads. They have nevertheless relied on this intuition to avoid overly crowded streets and to find them the most economic routes possible at certain hours of a day. Analysing their GPS trajectories can reveal the hidden knowledge valuable to determine the best time-varying route connecting two locations.

The overall approach divides into three steps: first to build an abstract graph modeling the real road network, to estimate the travel time of each road as a function of time, and finally to find the best route using a modified Dijkstra's algorithm. Since the first step demands the greatest creativity, I would like to highlight how I employed clustering technique to cope with the challenges in the first step.

With longitude and latitude as coordinates, each record in the trajectory database can be deemed a point in a 2D space. In order to model the real roads, the coordinates must be translated into road names, a process known as "reverse geo-encoding". However, this process is error prone: the headline picture gives a dramatic example where the green points resemble the entire road network in Beijing but are all claimed to be on the same road! With no reference to a map, automatically discovering the correct road that should have been mapped to amongst a set of erroneously mapped points is a central problem to solve. My solution rests upon the observation that majority of the points are correctly translated and congregate along the road they represent. Visually discernable as this pattern may be, it is hard to quantitatively demarcate correct points from outliers, since road boundaries are unknown. Clustering turns out to be able to approximate the boundaries by the positions of centroids. Each centroid can be considered as the median position of a group of points. When majority of the points scatter around the actual road, their median must be on the road. Indeed, when implementated as a 10x10 self-organising feature map, clustering corrals majority of the neurons into an amazing line that overlays the actual road (as shown in blue)!

Struggle of the Captive. When will the labyrinth master come to its rescue?

Design a robot with sensory apparatus to explore a randomly constructed maze

Whilst fulfilling course requirements, this project is also designed to be a competition. Each team must design a robot that achieves two goals: to explore the arena as thorough as possible and to move between two designated locations as quickly as possible. The arena consists of randomly scattered polymer blocks detectable by the infrared proximity sensors installed on each robot. For the exploration task, full credits are given to the team whose robot is able to avoid and describe the location of each "obstacle". The headline video shows the software I developed to simulate and control the physical robot. Obstacles are shown in black and the yellow region represents the already explored region. Teams are ranked, from the least to the most, by the time their robots spend travelling from the blue area to the red in the second task.

While proud of every aspect of this project, I'd like to highlight my novel exploration algorithm that granted my team the fastest exploration amongst all 20 teams. To ensure a thorough exploration, it works in a depth-first manner - that is, continuing to explore until backtracking - but differs from depth-first search in that it doesn't blindly follow one direction in the search tree. Instead, it evaluates the four directions available at each decision point and chooses the best. The main criteria include movability, whether the direction is blocked by obstacles, and reachability, whether the unexplored space can only be reached in this direction. While obviously a non-movable direction will never be a choice, the direction serving as the only nexus to a hinterland will definitely be cherished. Moreover, my algorithm has a special backtracking facility. In contrast to software implementation where backtracking is nothing but poping a stack, hardware implementation entails costly physical movement of the robot. As the explored regions get connected, there may exist a much shorter, at worst equal-length, path to the next decision point than the original backtracking path. My algorithm surpasses others by using A* algorithm to find the optimal backtracking path instead of reversing the stack.

In case you're not familiar with The Little Prince, it's actually a French novella Le Petit Prince written by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry.

My contributions to this project are mainly on the following two core functions.

  • A robot controller to issue instructions. Our robot is like a toddler: it can walk around and sense the world but relies on the controller to tell it what to do. The controller is connected to the robot remotely via Transmission Control Protocol. Moreover, it provides a robot simulator (as shown above) that issues commands to a virtual robot, which allowed us to quickly test alternative algorithms before the physical robot was built.

  • An intelligent exploration algorithm based on Depth-first search. When the robot gets stuck in the maze, it needs to backtrack. It's easier to implement backtracking in software by just poping from the stack which in hardware, however, means the robot has to physically (costly) reverse along the previous path. As the maze gets explored, a shorter backtracking path may exist. Therefore, we used A* search algorithm to find such a path, which saved us tremendous time.
